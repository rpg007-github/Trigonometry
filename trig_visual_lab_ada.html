Before We Start: What Even IS Trigonometry?

New Word

Trigonometry literally means "triangle measurement." It is the study of how the angles (the "corners") and the sides (the straight edges) of triangles are connected to each other.

Here is the big idea in one sentence: If you know one angle and one side of a certain type of triangle, you can figure out every other side. That is the entire subject.

💡 Think of it this way: Imagine you are standing 20 feet from a tall tree. You tilt your head up at a 60-degree angle to see the top. You have never climbed that tree. You have no tape measure long enough to reach the top. But with trig, you can calculate that the tree is about 34.6 feet tall, just from the angle and the distance you paced off. Angles let you find distances you cannot measure directly. That is the superpower trig gives you.

Two Words You Need Right Now

New Word

Angle = the amount of rotation between two lines that share the same starting point. Think of opening a door: the wider you push it, the bigger the angle. We measure angles in degrees (a full circle = 360°) or radians (a full circle = about 6.28, which is exactly 2π). Do not worry about radians yet. For now, just think in degrees.

New Word

Right triangle = a triangle where one of the three corners is exactly 90° (a perfect square corner, like the corner of a piece of paper or a book). All of trig starts here because right triangles have a special property: the ratios of their sides are always the same for a given angle, no matter how big or small the triangle is.

✓ Quick Check

A right triangle always has one angle that equals:

Correct! A right triangle always has one 90° angle. That is the "right" in "right triangle." It looks like a perfect square corner, like the corner of a book.

The One Thing You MUST Memorize

SOH - CAH - TOA
Say it out loud: "so - kah - toe - ah"

Sine = Opposite / Hypotenuse
Cosine = Adjacent / Hypotenuse

You do not know what "opposite," "adjacent," or "hypotenuse" mean yet. That is totally fine! You will learn them on the very next tab. The point is: this one phrase unlocks almost everything in the first half of trig. Say it five times right now. Seriously. Out loud.

The Right Triangle

Everything in trig starts with one shape: the right triangle. Before we touch any formulas, you need to be able to look at a right triangle and name its three sides. There are only three names to learn, and they are always the same.

Step 1: Learn the Three Side Names

New Word: Hypotenuse

The hypotenuse (say "hi-POT-en-oose") is the longest side of a right triangle. It is always the side that sits across from the 90° angle. It never touches the 90° corner. In the triangle below, it is the dark/black line.

Memory trick: "Hypotenuse" is the longest word, and it is the longest side.

New Word: Opposite Side

The opposite side is the side that is directly across from the angle you are looking at. We call that angle θ (the Greek letter "theta," pronounced "THAY-tuh"). The opposite side does NOT touch the angle θ at all. In the triangle below, it is the red line.

Think: if you are standing at the angle θ and looking straight ahead, the opposite side is the wall you are staring at.

New Word: Adjacent Side

The adjacent side is the side that is right next to the angle θ. It touches the angle, but it is NOT the hypotenuse. In the triangle below, it is the blue line.

"Adjacent" just means "next to." It is the floor you are standing on if you are standing at angle θ.

✓ Quick Check

Which side of a right triangle is always the longest?

Yes! The hypotenuse is always the longest side. It is always across from the 90° angle. The other two sides ("legs") can change depending on which angle you are looking at.

Step 3: Connect the Sides to the Formulas

Remember SOH-CAH-TOA? Now you know what all the words in it mean! Sine, cosine, and tangent are just ratios (fractions) of two sides of the triangle:

sin(θ) = Opposite / Hypotenuse   ← SOH
cos(θ) = Adjacent / Hypotenuse   ← CAH
tan(θ) = Opposite / Adjacent     ← TOA
💡 Think of it this way: These are just fractions. Nothing fancy. sin(θ) answers the question: "What fraction of the hypotenuse is the opposite side?" If sin(30°) = 0.5, that means the opposite side is exactly half as long as the hypotenuse. That is it. No magic.
👋 Try It Yourself

Do this right now: Set the angle slider to 30° and the hypotenuse to 10. Look at the readout: sin(30°) = 0.500. Now look at "Opposite" (should be 5.00) and "Hypotenuse" (should be 10). Check: 5 ÷ 10 = 0.5. That IS sin(30°). You just verified the formula with your own eyes!

Now try 60°: Notice sin(60°) = 0.866, which was the value of cos(30°)! Sine and cosine "swap" when the angles add to 90°.

✓ Quick Check

Set the slider to 45°. What is tan(45°)?

Correct! At 45°, the opposite and adjacent sides are the same length (both 7.07). Tangent = opposite / adjacent = 7.07 / 7.07 = 1. Any number divided by itself equals 1.

Worked Examples: Finding Missing Sides

These are the most common type of problem you will see. There are only three things you can be given, and only three things you can be asked to find. Here are all the key patterns:

Finding a Missing Side Using SOH (Given: angle = 30°, hypotenuse = 10, find the opposite side)
Step 1: Label what you know and want Known: angle θ = 30°, hypotenuse = 10. Want: opposite side. Underline or circle these in a real problem.
Step 2: Which SOH-CAH-TOA formula has both things you know? I know the hypotenuse and want the opposite. SOH uses opposite AND hypotenuse. Use SOH.
sin(θ) = Opposite / Hypotenuse
Step 3: Plug in what you know
sin(30°) = Opposite / 10
Look up or recall: sin(30°) = 0.5
0.5 = Opposite / 10
Step 4: Solve for the unknown (multiply both sides by 10)
Opposite = 0.5 × 10 = 5
Answer The opposite side is 5 units. Set the slider to 30° and verify: Opposite = 5.00 ✓
Finding the Hypotenuse Using CAH (Given: angle = 40°, adjacent = 8, find the hypotenuse)
Step 1: Label what you know and want Known: angle θ = 40°, adjacent = 8. Want: hypotenuse. CAH uses adjacent AND hypotenuse. Use CAH.
Step 2: Write the formula
cos(θ) = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
cos(40°) = 8 / Hypotenuse
Step 3: The unknown is in the denominator. Cross-multiply.
Hypotenuse × cos(40°) = 8
Hypotenuse = 8 / cos(40°)
cos(40°) ≈ 0.766
Hypotenuse = 8 / 0.766 ≈ 10.44
Answer The hypotenuse is approximately 10.44 units. Rule: if the unknown is on the bottom of a fraction, divide both sides by the trig value.
Finding an Angle Using Inverse Trig (Given: opposite = 6, hypotenuse = 10, find the angle)
Step 1: Identify what you know We have opposite and hypotenuse. That is SOH. But we want the angle, not a side.
sin(θ) = 6 / 10 = 0.6
Step 2: Reverse it using "inverse sine" To find the angle when you know the sine value, use the inverse function. On a calculator: sin¹ button (often labeled sin⁻¹ or arcsin).
θ = sin¹(0.6)
Answer
θ = sin¹(0.6) ≈ 36.87°
The angle is about 36.9°. sin¹ does NOT mean 1/sin. It means "what angle has this sine value?" See the Identities tab for more on this.
✓ Quick Check

You know the angle and the adjacent side, and want the hypotenuse. Which formula?

Right! CAH uses adjacent and hypotenuse. Rearrange: Hypotenuse = Adjacent / cos(θ).

The "Flip" Functions (Reciprocals)

Sometimes you need to flip a fraction upside down. Instead of opposite/hypotenuse, you need hypotenuse/opposite. These flipped versions have their own names:

New Words

"Reciprocal" means flip the fraction:

Cosecant (csc) = 1/sin = Hypotenuse / Opposite

Secant (sec) = 1/cos = Hypotenuse / Adjacent

Cotangent (cot) = 1/tan = Adjacent / Opposite

⚠️ Watch Out! Everyone gets this wrong at first: Cosecant goes with sine (not cosine!). Secant goes with cosine (not sine!). The names deliberately cross. csc pairs with sin. sec pairs with cos.
✓ Quick Check

The reciprocal of cosine is:

Yes! sec = 1/cos. The names cross on purpose: secant goes with cosine, cosecant goes with sine.

The Unit Circle

In the triangle tab, trig only worked for angles between 0° and 90° (because those are the only angles inside a right triangle). But what about 120°? Or 270°? The unit circle extends trig to work for any angle.

What Is It?

New Concept

The unit circle is a circle with a radius of exactly 1, centered at the point (0, 0) on a graph. "Unit" just means "one."

The key idea: Every point on this circle is (cosθ, sinθ). Cosine = the x-coordinate (horizontal position). Sine = the y-coordinate (vertical position). That is the whole concept.

💡 Think of it this way: Imagine a ladybug on a clock face, starting at 3 o'clock. She walks counterclockwise. At any moment, her horizontal distance from center is cos(θ) and her vertical distance from center is sin(θ). You are about to drag the point around the circle and be that ladybug.
✓ Quick Check

On the unit circle, cosine tells you the point's:

Yes! Cosine = x (horizontal). Sine = y (vertical). The blue line in the diagram shows cosine, the red line shows sine.

What Are Radians?

New Word

Radians = another way to measure angles. One full circle = 360° = 2π radians (≈ 6.28). Half circle = 180° = π. Quarter = 90° = π/2.

To convert: degrees × (π/180) = radians.   Radians × (180/π) = degrees.

Convert 270° to radians
Formula
Radians = Degrees × (π / 180)
Plug in
Radians = 270 × (π / 180) = (270/180)π = (3/2)π
Answer 270° = 3π/2 radians ≈ 4.71. Verify: drag to the bottom of the unit circle. Radians shows "1.50π" or 3π/2.
Convert 5π/6 radians to degrees
Formula
Degrees = Radians × (180 / π)
Plug in
Degrees = (5π/6) × (180/π) = (5 × 180) / 6 = 900 / 6
Answer 5π/6 radians = 150°. The π's cancel out. You are left with simple arithmetic.
✓ Quick Check

180° equals how many radians?

Yes! 180° = π radians. This is the most important conversion to memorize. Try: 180 × (π/180) = π.

The Four Quadrants

The circle is divided into four sections called quadrants. Knowing the quadrant tells you whether sin and cos are positive or negative.

"All Students Take Calculus" = which functions are positive:

QI (top-right, 0°–90°) = ALL positive
QII (top-left, 90°–180°) = only SINE positive
QIII (bottom-left, 180°–270°) = only TANGENT positive
QIV (bottom-right, 270°–360°) = only COSINE positive
Find sin(210°) and cos(210°) without a calculator
Step 1: Find the quadrant 210° is between 180° and 270° → QIII. In QIII, only tangent is positive. Both sin and cos are negative.
Step 2: Find the reference angle QIII formula: reference = angle − 180° = 210 − 180 = 30°.
Step 3: Look up the reference angle values sin(30°) = 1/2. cos(30°) = √3/2. These are your "raw" numbers.
Step 4: Apply the QIII signs (both negative) sin(210°) = −1/2 = −0.5
cos(210°) = −√3/2 ≈ −0.866
Verify: drag to 210° on the unit circle. You should see exactly these values.
👋 Try It

Drag to about 150° (QII). Sine is positive (~0.5) but cosine is negative (~−0.866). That matches "Students" = only Sine positive in QII.

✓ Quick Check

At 240° (QIII), which function is positive?

Correct! QIII = "Take" = Tangent. Both sin and cos are negative, but negative divided by negative = positive tangent.

Reference Angles

New Concept

A reference angle is the acute angle (under 90°) between your angle's line and the x-axis. Same trig values, just possibly different signs.

How to find it: QII: 180° − angle.   QIII: angle − 180°.   QIV: 360° − angle.

✓ Quick Check

Reference angle for 225°?

Yes! 225° is in QIII. Reference = 225 − 180 = 45°. So sin(225°) and cos(225°) have the same numbers as 45°, but both negative (QIII).

Wave Graphs

When you dragged the point around the unit circle, you saw sine go up and down like a wave. If you "unrolled" that circular motion into a straight line, you get the sine wave: one of the most important shapes in all of math and science. Sound, light, ocean tides, even your heartbeat, all make this shape.

Here you will build a wave from scratch using four sliders. Change ONE slider at a time so you can see exactly what each one does.

The Four Controls

What each slider does

Amplitude (A) = How tall the wave is from the middle to the top. |A| is always positive. Negative A flips the wave upside down.

B value (frequency) = How many complete cycles fit in a standard space. Period = 2π / |B|. Bigger B = shorter period = more waves packed together.

Phase shift (C) = How far the wave slides left or right. Shift = −C/B.

Vertical shift (D) = How far the wave moves up or down. Changes the midline (center line) of the wave.

Trig Identities

An identity is an equation that is always true, no matter what angle you plug in. It is not something you solve. It is a fact you use to rewrite expressions in different forms.

💡 Think of it this way: $1.00 = 4 quarters = 10 dimes = 100 pennies. Same value, different look. You pick whichever form is most useful. Trig identities work the same way.

The Most Important Identity

sin²(θ) + cos²(θ) = 1

The Pythagorean Identity. ALWAYS true. Every angle. No exceptions.
Wait, what does sin² mean?

sin²(θ) = [sin(θ)]². Find sine first, THEN square the result. Example: sin²(30°) = (0.5)² = 0.25.

Why does it work?

On the unit circle, point = (cosθ, sinθ), distance from center = 1. Pythagorean theorem: x² + y² = r². Since r = 1: cos²θ + sin²θ = 1.

👋 Verify

Go to the Unit Circle tab. Pick any angle. Square sin and cos, add them. You get 1. Try 30°: (0.5)² + (0.866)² = 0.25 + 0.75 = 1.0. Try 210°: (−0.5)² + (−0.866)² = 0.25 + 0.75 = 1.0. Always works!

✓ Quick Check

If sin(θ) = 0.6, what is cos²(θ)?

Yes! (0.6)² + cos² = 1 → 0.36 + cos² = 1 → cos² = 0.64.

Rearrangements

sin²(θ) = 1 − cos²(θ)
cos²(θ) = 1 − sin²(θ)

Same identity, just rearranged. These come up constantly in simplification problems.

Other Pythagorean Identities

Divide sin² + cos² = 1 by cos² to get one; divide by sin² to get the other:

1 + tan²(θ) = sec²(θ)
1 + cot²(θ) = csc²(θ)

Worked Examples: Using Identities

sin(θ) = 3/5, θ in QI. Find cos(θ).
Step 1sin² + cos² = 1
Step 2(3/5)² + cos² = 1 → 9/25 + cos² = 1
Step 3cos² = 1 − 9/25 = 16/25 → cos = ±4/5
Step 4QI = all positive → cos(θ) = 4/5 = 0.8
Simplify: (1 − cos²x) / sin(x)
Step 1: Recognize the numerator 1 − cos²x = sin²x (this IS the Pythagorean identity rearranged)
(1 − cos²x) / sin(x) = sin²x / sin(x)
Step 2: Cancel one sine from top and bottom
sin²x / sinx = sinx
Answer sin(x). The entire expression simplifies to just sine.
Prove: tan(x) × cos(x) = sin(x)
Strategy: Work on one side only Take the left side: tan(x) × cos(x). Replace tan(x) with its definition.
tan(x) = sin(x)/cos(x)
Substitute
[sin(x)/cos(x)] × cos(x)
The cos(x) on top and bottom cancel.
Result
= sin(x)
Left side = right side. Identity proven. Always start with the more complicated side and simplify toward the simpler side.

Sum, Difference, and Double-Angle

⚠️ Watch Out! sin(A + B) ≠ sin(A) + sin(B)! Quick proof: sin(30+60) = sin(90) = 1. But sin(30)+sin(60) = 0.5 + 0.866 = 1.366. Not equal! You must use the formulas below.
Sum/Difference:
sin(A ± B) = sinA cosB ± cosA sinB
cos(A ± B) = cosA cosB ∓ sinA sinB

Double-Angle:
sin(2θ) = 2 sinθ cosθ
cos(2θ) = cos²θ − sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 = 1 − 2sin²θ

Half-Angle:
sin(θ/2) = ±√[(1 − cosθ)/2]
cos(θ/2) = ±√[(1 + cosθ)/2]
If sinθ = 3/5 (QI), find sin(2θ) and cos(2θ)
Step 1: We already know sin = 3/5. Find cos first. From previous example: cosθ = 4/5 (QI, positive).
Step 2: Apply sin(2θ) = 2 sinθ cosθ
sin(2θ) = 2 × (3/5) × (4/5) = 24/25
Step 3: Apply cos(2θ) = 1 − 2sin²θ
cos(2θ) = 1 − 2(3/5)² = 1 − 2(9/25) = 1 − 18/25 = 7/25
Answers sin(2θ) = 24/25, cos(2θ) = 7/25
✓ Quick Check

sin(2θ) equals:

Yes! sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ)cos(θ). The double-angle formula. Note: 2sin(θ) alone is NOT the same thing.

Solving Trig Equations

A trig equation asks: "For what angle(s) is this true?" Because trig functions repeat (waves go forever), there are usually multiple answers. The method is always the same three steps:

The 3-Step Method

Step 1: Isolate the trig function (get sin(x) or cos(x) alone on one side).

Step 2: Find the reference angle (what QI angle gives that value?).

Step 3: Find ALL solutions (which quadrants work? use ASTC).

Solve: 2sin(x) − 1 = 0 on [0, 2π)
Step 1: Isolate sin
2sin(x) = 1 → sin(x) = 1/2
Step 2: Reference angle sin(?) = 1/2 → reference = 30° = π/6 This is a "clean" value from the special triangles. Memorize it!
Step 3: Quadrants where sine is positive ASTC: sine positive in QI and QII.
QI: x = π/6
QII: x = π − π/6 = 5π/6
Answerx = π/6 and x = 5π/6
Solve: cos(x) = −1/2 on [0, 2π)
Step 1: Already isolatedcos(x) = −1/2
Step 2: Reference angle cos(?) = 1/2 (ignore the negative for now) → reference = 60° = π/3
Step 3: Cosine is NEGATIVE in QII and QIII QII: x = π − π/3 = 2π/3
QIII: x = π + π/3 = 4π/3
Answerx = 2π/3 and x = 4π/3
Solve: 2cos²(x) − cos(x) − 1 = 0 on [0, 2π)
Step 1: Spot the quadratic Let u = cos(x). Then: 2u² − u − 1 = 0. A "quadratic" = equation with u². Solve by factoring or the quadratic formula.
Step 2: Factor
(2u+1)(u−1)=0 → u=−1/2 or u=1
So cos(x) = −1/2 or cos(x) = 1.
Step 3a: Solve cos(x) = −1/2 Reference = 60°. Negative in QII, QIII: x = 2π/3, 4π/3
Step 3b: Solve cos(x) = 1 cos = 1 only at x = 0 (right side of unit circle).
Answerx = 0, 2π/3, 4π/3
Find ALL solutions (not just 0 to 2π) of sin(x) = √3/2
Step 1: Reference angle sin(?) = √3/2 → reference = 60° = π/3
Step 2: Solutions in [0, 2π) QI: x = π/3. QII: x = π − π/3 = 2π/3
Step 3: Add all repeating cycles using + 2πk
x = π/3 + 2πk,   or   x = 2π/3 + 2πk
where k is any integer (0, ±1, ±2, ...). This gives all solutions from negative infinity to positive infinity.
✓ Quick Check

Solving cos(x) = −1/2. Cosine is negative in which quadrants?

Correct! Cosine is positive in QI and QIV only. So it is NEGATIVE in QII and QIII.
⚠️ Watch Out! Never divide by a trig function! sin(x)cos(x) = sin(x) → do NOT divide both sides by sin(x). You lose the solutions where sin(x) = 0. Instead: sin(x)cos(x) − sin(x) = 0 → sin(x)[cos(x) − 1] = 0. Factor it!

Law of Sines and Law of Cosines

SOH-CAH-TOA only works for right triangles. For any other triangle, use these two laws.

Which One Do I Use?

Decision Rule

Law of Sines: Use when you have a matching pair (a side AND the angle directly across from it). Cases: AAS, ASA, SSA.

Law of Cosines: Use when you do NOT have a matching pair. Cases: SAS, SSS.

💡 Think of it this way: Law of Sines is a "matching" tool: needs a pair to start. Law of Cosines is "brute force": works without a pair. Fun fact: if C = 90°, cos(90°) = 0, and Law of Cosines becomes c² = a² + b²: just the Pythagorean theorem!
Law of Sines: a/sin(A) = b/sin(B) = c/sin(C)

Law of Cosines: c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos(C)

Area: Area = (1/2)ab sin(C)   (C = angle BETWEEN sides a and b)
✓ Quick Check

You know two sides and the angle BETWEEN them (SAS). Which law?

Correct! SAS = no matching pair → Law of Cosines.
Law of Sines: A = 40°, B = 60°, a = 10. Find b.
Identify matching pairs Pair 1: side a = 10 with angle A = 40°. Pair 2: side b (unknown) with angle B = 60°.
Set up Law of Sines
a/sin(A) = b/sin(B) → 10/sin(40°) = b/sin(60°)
Solve for b (cross multiply)
b = 10 × sin(60°) / sin(40°) = 10 × 0.866 / 0.643
Answer b ≈ 13.47
Law of Cosines: a = 7, b = 10, C = 50°. Find c.
Identify the case We know two sides (a, b) and the angle BETWEEN them (C). This is SAS → Law of Cosines.
Write the formula
c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos(C)
c² = 7² + 10² − 2(7)(10)cos(50°)
Calculate
c² = 49 + 100 − 140(0.643) = 149 − 90 = 59
Answer c = √59 ≈ 7.68
Find the area of a triangle with sides a = 5, b = 8, and included angle C = 35°
Formula
Area = (1/2) × a × b × sin(C)
Plug in
Area = (1/2) × 5 × 8 × sin(35°) = 20 × 0.574
Answer Area ≈ 11.47 square units C must be the angle that sits BETWEEN sides a and b. Not just any angle.

Vectors: Direction + Distance

This is where trig shows up outside of pure math. If you have ever said "go 3 blocks east and 4 blocks north," you already used a vector.

New Words

Scalar: A number with only size. "55 mph." No direction.

Vector: A number with size AND direction. "55 mph north."

Components: The horizontal (x) and vertical (y) pieces. Written <x, y>.

💡 Think of it this way: "Walk 5 blocks" = scalar. "Walk 3 east and 4 north" = vector. The 3 and 4 are components. Total: √(9+16) = 5 blocks (Pythagorean theorem). Direction: tan¹(4/3) ≈ 53° north of east (trig). Vectors = Pythagorean theorem + trig!
Magnitude: |v| = √(x² + y²)
Direction: θ = tan¹(y/x)   (check quadrant!)

Components from magnitude + angle:
x = |v| cos(θ)    y = |v| sin(θ)

Dot product: u · v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂
Dot product = 0 → vectors are perpendicular.
Plane flies 300 mph at 35° north of east. Find x and y components.
East (x) component uses cosine
x = 300 cos(35°) = 300 × 0.819 = 245.7 mph
North (y) component uses sine
y = 300 sin(35°) = 300 × 0.574 = 172.1 mph
Verify magnitude
√(245.7² + 172.1²) = √(60369 + 29618) = √89987 ≈ 300 ✓
A vector has components <4, 3>. Find its magnitude and direction angle.
Magnitude
|v| = √(4² + 3²) = √(16 + 9) = √25 = 5
Direction angle
θ = tan¹(3/4) = tan¹(0.75) ≈ 36.87°
Both components positive → QI. No quadrant adjustment needed.
Answer Magnitude = 5, direction = 36.87° above the positive x-axis.
Are vectors u = <3, 4> and v = <4, −3> perpendicular?
Compute the dot product
u · v = (3)(4) + (4)(−3) = 12 − 12 = 0
Conclusion Dot product = 0 → Yes, the vectors are perpendicular. This is the fastest way to check perpendicularity. No angles needed.
✓ Quick Check

Vector: magnitude 50, angle 60°. The x-component is:

Yes! x always uses cosine. x = |v|cos(θ) = 50 × cos(60°) = 50 × 0.5 = 25. (y = 50sin(60°) = 43.3)
